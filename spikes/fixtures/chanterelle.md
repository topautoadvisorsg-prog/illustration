## CHANTERELLE *Cantharellus spp.* | EDIBLE

> The chanterelle is gold — not metaphorically. It is the color of late October sunlight
> filtered through hickory leaves, and it grows out of the moss on the forest floor as
> if some quiet, slow alchemy had been at work beneath the duff for weeks before you
> arrived. It is one of the great wild foods of the Northeast, and one of the few that
> a careful beginner can learn to identify with high confidence.

### WHAT IT IS
One of the most prized edible wild mushrooms in the world. A mycorrhizal species that
forms symbiotic relationships with oak, hemlock, and birch — which is why you will
never grow chanterelles in a basement. They appear in mid to late summer through
early fall, in mixed hardwood and boreal forests across the Northeast, often in
loose troops scattered across mossy ground.

### HOW TO IDENTIFY
- **Cap:** 1–5 inches across, golden to deep egg-yolk yellow, with a wavy and lobed
  margin. Smooth, slightly silky to the touch. Often funnel-shaped or vase-shaped as
  the mushroom matures.
- **Stem:** Solid (not hollow), tapering downward, same color as the cap or slightly
  paler, 1–3 inches tall.
- **"Gills":** This is the diagnostic feature. Chanterelles do not have true gills.
  Instead they have **false gills** — blunt, shallow, forking ridges that run down
  the stem. The ridges look more like wrinkles in wax than like the knife-thin gills
  of an ordinary mushroom.
- **Smell:** Distinctly fruity — apricot is the classic comparison. If you crouch and
  smell one in the field, you'll know it.
- **Spore print:** Pale yellow to cream.

### WHERE & WHEN
Mid-summer through early fall, after a soaking rain followed by warm days. Mixed
hardwood forests with oak, hemlock, and birch. Mossy ground, often along old logging
roads and stream banks. They tend to come up in the same spots year after year — when
you find a patch, mark it and come back.

### LOOK-ALIKE WARNING — Jack-o-lantern *(Omphalotus illudens)*
The jack-o-lantern is **toxic** — it will cause severe gastrointestinal illness. It
fruits in clusters at the base of dead or dying hardwoods (chanterelles never grow
in clusters from wood — they grow individually from soil). Critically, the jack-o-lantern
has **true, knife-thin gills**, not false-gill ridges. The flesh of a jack-o-lantern
is orange throughout; chanterelle flesh is pale white inside. If in any doubt, do not eat.

### EAT?
Yes — with confident identification. Sauté in butter with a small pinch of salt until
the water cooks off and the edges begin to crisp. Do not wash in standing water (they
absorb it and turn rubbery); brush off debris with a dry cloth instead.
